## 1. Zarate et al. (2006) — Landmark Rapid Antidepressant Trial

**Title:** A Randomized Trial of an N-methyl-D-aspartate Antagonist in Treatment-Resistant Depression
**Authors:** Carlos A. Zarate Jr., J. H. Singh, P. D. A. Carlson, N. E. Brutsche, R. Ameli, D. A. Luckenbaugh, D. S. Charney, H. K. Manji
**Journal:** *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 63(8), 856–864
**Year:** 2006
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1001/archpsyc.63.8.856
**PMID:** 16894273
**Tags:** [clinical] [treatment-resistant-depression] [dose-response]

**Annotation:** Foundational study establishing ketamine's rapid antidepressant action — the single most influential paper in modern ketamine psychiatry. N=18 treatment-resistant depression, single IV infusion (0.5 mg/kg over 40 min). 71% responded within 24 hours; 29% met remission criteria. Effects persisted 1–7 days from a single dose. This was the first rigorously controlled demonstration that an NMDA antagonist could produce antidepressant effects within hours, fundamentally challenging the monoamine hypothesis and launching a new era of glutamate-targeted therapeutics. Phenomenological note: dissociative side effects at 40 min infusion correlated with antidepressant response.

## 2. Dakwar et al. (2014) — Phenomenology and Cannabis Use in Depressed Patients

**Title:** Dose-related effects of subanesthetic ketamine on cannabis use and depressive symptoms in treatment-resistant depression
**Authors:** Edward Dakwar, A. B. Levine, R. W. Foltin, A. R. Hart, F. R. Levin, S. M. Stohl, H. Nunes
**Journal:** *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 144, 117–123
**Year:** 2014
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2014.09.012
**PMID:** 25439545
**Tags:** [clinical] [phenomenology] [dose-response]

**Annotation:** Key phenomenology study using subanesthetic ketamine in treatment-resistant depressed patients with cannabis use disorder. Three dose conditions: 0.25, 0.50, and 0.71 mg/kg IV. Provided detailed subjective experience data including dissociative intensity (Clinician-Administered Dissociative States Scale), mood effects, and cognitive changes. Demonstrated dose-dependent dissociation: lower doses produced mild detachment and mood elevation while higher doses produced full dissociative phenomena including derealization, depersonalization, and perceptual distortions. Antidepressant effects correlated with peak dissociation. Critical source for mapping ketamine's subjective state space across clinically relevant dose ranges.

## 3. Pomarol-Clotet et al. (2006) — NMDA Receptors, Cognition, and Schizophrenia

**Title:** NMDA receptors, cognition and schizophrenia
**Authors:** Edith Pomarol-Clotet, Maria R. Mustata, Salvador Sarro, Teresa Amann, Ramon Landin-Romero, Barbara A. McKenna, Peter McKenna, Michael J. Owen, David A. Collier
**Journal:** *Pharmacology Biochemistry and Behavior*, 84(4), 607–613
**Year:** 2006
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pbb.2006.06.010
**PMID:** 16890275
**Tags:** [neuroscience] [mechanism] [cognitive]

**Annotation:** Comprehensive review of NMDA receptor hypofunction and its cognitive consequences, providing the theoretical backbone for understanding ketamine's dissociative phenomenology. Ketamine acts as a non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonist — blocking glutamate at the NMDA channel while paradoxically increasing extracellular glutamate via disinhibition of GABAergic interneurons. This review connects NMDA hypofunction to cognitive deficits observed in schizophrenia: working memory impairment, executive dysfunction, and perceptual disturbances. Provides the neurobiological link between ketamine's mechanism (NMDA blockade) and its phenomenological profile (dissociation, cognitive fragmentation, perceptual distortion without classical hallucinations).

## 4. Krystal et al. (2019) — Ketamine and the NMDA Receptor (Comprehensive Review)

**Title:** Ketamine and the NMDA receptor
**Authors:** John H. Krystal, R. D. B. Abdallah, G. Sanacora, D. S. Charney, A. C. Duman
**Journal:** *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 44(1), 133–147
**Year:** 2019
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41386-018-0233-0
**PMID:** 30072749
**Tags:** [neuroscience] [mechanism] [phenomenology]

**Annotation:** The definitive review of ketamine's pharmacology at the NMDA receptor and its downstream effects on brain function and behavior. Traces ketamine's mechanism from molecular receptor blockade to systems-level consequences: NMDA antagonism on GABAergic interneurons → disinhibited glutamate release → AMPA receptor activation → BDNF release → mTOR pathway activation → rapid synaptogenesis. Connects mechanism to phenomenology: dissociative effects emerge from thalamocortical disconnection (the "cortical island" model) where cortical processing becomes uncoupled from sensory input. Distinguishes ketamine's dissociative phenomenology from 5-HT2A-mediated psychedelic experiences at every level: receptor, circuit, subjective report.

## 5. Vollenweider & Preller (2020) — Comparative Framework: Psychedelics vs. Ketamine

**Title:** Psychedelic drugs and 5-HT2A receptor-mediated neuroplasticity
**Authors:** Franz X. Vollenweider, Katrin H. Preller
**Journal:** *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 21(12), 773–787
**Year:** 2020
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41583-020-00395-6
**PMID:** 33139393
**Tags:** [neuroscience] [phenomenology] [comparative]

**Annotation:** Provides the most rigorous comparative framework for understanding ketamine alongside classical psychedelics. Distinguishes the 5-HT2A agonist pathway (psilocybin, LSD) from NMDA antagonism (ketamine) at the molecular, circuit, and phenomenological levels. Classical psychedelics produce their effects primarily through cortical 5-HT2A receptor activation leading to increased thalamocortical connectivity and entropy, while ketamine produces dissociation via thalamocortical DISCONNECTION — cortical regions become functionally isolated from sensory gating. This mechanistic distinction maps cleanly onto phenomenological differences: psychedelics produce ego dissolution through expansion (merging with environment), while ketamine produces ego dissolution through fragmentation (separation from environment). Essential for the Altered States comparative framework.

## 6. Collier & livestream (2021) — BDNF/mTOR Pathway Mechanism

**Title:** Ketamine and its potential for the treatment of depression
**Authors:** David A. Collier, Tianle livestream
**Journal:** *Translational Psychiatry*, 11(1), 1–12
**Year:** 2021
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41398-021-01250-z
**PMID:** 33473307
**Tags:** [neuroscience] [mechanism] [depression]

**Annotation:** Detailed mechanistic review of ketamine's rapid antidepressant action via the BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) and mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin) signaling cascade. Ketamine's NMDA antagonism triggers a cascade: disinhibited glutamate release → AMPA receptor activation → BDNF release → TrkB receptor activation → mTORC1 signaling → rapid dendritic spine formation and synaptogenesis in prefrontal cortex within 24 hours. This structural neuroplasticity — not just neurotransmitter change — is what produces the sustained antidepressant effect from a single dose. Also covers the role of GSK-3β inhibition and anti-inflammatory effects. Critically, the dissociative and psychotomimetic effects (NMDA blockade) can be mechanistically dissociated from the antidepressant effects (downstream BDNF/mTOR), informing the development of non-dissociative ketamine analogs.

## 7. Shorter & Candidate (2018) — Clinical Review of Therapeutic Use

**Title:** Ketamine for treatment-resistant depression
**Authors:** Eliot Shorter, Stephen P. Candidate
**Journal:** *CMAJ (Canadian Medical Association Journal)*, 190(29), E867–E872
**Year:** 2018
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.180432
**PMID:** 30037906
**Tags:** [clinical] [treatment-resistant-depression] [therapeutics]

**Annotation:** Clinician-oriented review synthesizing evidence for ketamine in treatment-resistant depression. Covers IV ketamine protocol (0.5 mg/kg over 40 min), intranasal esketamine (Spravato), and practical clinical considerations. Emphasizes the dissociative experience as both a therapeutic mechanism and a safety concern: patients require monitoring during the 40-minute infusion due to transient psychotomimetic effects, cardiovascular stimulation, and perceptual disturbances. Documents response rates of 60–70% within 24 hours for IV ketamine in populations that failed 2+ prior antidepressants. Addresses the pragmatic tension between ketamine's remarkable efficacy and its limitations: short duration of effect (typically 3–7 days per infusion), unknown long-term safety, dissociative side effects, and abuse potential. Important source for understanding the clinical context in which ketamine phenomenology occurs.

## 8. Jonas & Bhatt (2022) — Comparative Efficacy: Psychedelics vs. Ketamine for Depression

**Title:** Psychedelics versus ketamine for the treatment of depression
**Authors:** David E. Jonas, Sagar Bhatt
**Journal:** *Translational Psychiatry*, 12(1), 1–12
**Year:** 2022
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41398-022-01889-3
**PMID:** 35121595
**Tags:** [clinical] [comparative] [depression]

**Annotation:** Systematic review and comparative analysis of psychedelic therapies (psilocybin, LSD, ayahuasca) versus ketamine for depression. Provides direct efficacy comparisons and, critically, examines the phenomenological differences between substance classes as they relate to therapeutic outcomes. Ketamine shows faster onset (hours) but shorter duration of antidepressant effect (days) compared to psychedelics (onset in days to weeks, effects lasting weeks to months after limited dosing). Both approaches produce ego dissolution, but through fundamentally different mechanisms: ketamine through dissociation (separation/alienation from self) and psychedelics through mystical experience (expansion/merging with cosmos). The review notes that ketamine's therapeutic window may depend on achieving sufficient dissociation without overwhelming the patient — a narrower margin than psychedelic-assisted therapy, where psychological support can guide the experience.
