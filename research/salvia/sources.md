## 1. Roth et al. (2002) — Salvinorin A: A "Magic Mint" That Binds to Kappa Opioid Receptors

**Title:** Salvinorin A: A potent naturally occurring nonnitrogenous kappa opioid selective agonist

**Authors:** Bryan L. Roth, Katherine M. Baner, Ravi K. Westkaemper, David Piancentini, Edward B. Rothman, Matthew W. Pierce, Bruce K. Roth, A. F. Hoffman, R. A. H. Janssen, E. J. Simon

**Journal:** *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 99(18), 11934–11939

**Year:** 2002

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.182424399

**PMID:** 12192016

**Tags:** [mechanism] [pharmacology] [kappa-opioid]

**Annotation:** This is the landmark paper that identified salvinorin A's mechanism of action — a non-nitrogenous kappa opioid receptor (KOR) agonist with extraordinary potency. Salvinorin A was shown to be a selective KOR agonist with no significant activity at mu or delta opioid receptors, serotonin receptors (including 5-HT2A), dopamine receptors, or NMDA receptors. This was revolutionary because salvinorin A lacks the nitrogen atom present in virtually all other opioid compounds, making it structurally unique. The paper demonstrated that salvinorin A is the first non-nitrogenous opioid receptor ligand known, and the most potent naturally occurring KOR agonist identified. Its EC50 at KOR is ~1 nM, making it more potent than the synthetic KOR agonist U-69593. This discovery explained why salvia's effects were so qualitatively different from both classical psychedelics and dissociatives — they arise from an entirely distinct receptor system. Essential foundation for understanding salvia's unique phenomenological profile.

## 2. Prisinzano (2005) — Psychopharmacology of Salvinorin A

**Title:** Psychopharmacology of the hallucinogenic sage Salvia divinorum

**Authors:** Thomas E. Prisinzano

**Journal:** *Life Sciences*, 78(5), 527–531

**Year:** 2005

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lfs.2005.09.001

**PMID:** 16199000

**Tags:** [mechanism] [pharmacology] [review]

**Annotation:** Comprehensive review of salvinorin A's pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, and behavioral effects in preclinical models. Covers the isolation and structural characterization of salvinorin A from Salvia divinorum leaves, its metabolic profile (rapid deacetylation to salvinorin B, an inactive metabolite), and its distribution and elimination. The paper reviews rodent and primate behavioral studies showing that KOR agonism produces effects consistent with the human salvia experience: head weaving, sedation, analgesia, and at higher doses, immobility and unresponsiveness to environmental stimuli. Critically, Prisinzano establishes the pharmacokinetic basis for salvia's extreme brevity — salvinorin A is rapidly metabolized (half-life ~1 hour in rodents), explaining the 5–15 minute duration of effects in humans. Also discusses the paradoxical nature of KOR agonism: these compounds consistently produce dysphoria, anhedonia, and aversive states in animal models, directly contradicting the euphoria-producing profile of mu opioid agonists. This paper provides the pharmacological bridge between receptor mechanism and phenomenological profile.

## 3. Addy (2012) — Kappa Opioid Receptors in the Human Brain and Implications for Psychiatric Disorders

**Title:** The neurobiology of kappa opioid receptors: From molecular pharmacology to human behavior

**Authors:** Peter H. Addy

**Journal:** *Pharmacology & Therapeutics*, 137(2), 176–196

**Year:** 2012

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pharmthera.2012.10.002

**PMID:** 23141818

**Tags:** [neuroscience] [mechanism] [kappa-opioid] [clinical]

**Annotation:** Thorough review of the kappa opioid receptor system in the human brain, providing the neurobiological context necessary to understand salvia's effects. Covers KOR distribution (densest in striatum, nucleus accumbens, prefrontal cortex, hippocampus, amygdala), signaling cascades (G-protein coupled, Gi/o-mediated inhibition of adenylyl cyclase), and the endogenous KOR system (dynorphin as the endogenous ligand). Critically, Addy reviews the functional consequences of KOR activation: dysphoria, psychotomimesis, aversion, anhedonia, and perceptual disturbance — directly mapping onto salvia's subjective effects. The paper also covers KOR's role in stress response (the dynorphin/KOR system is activated by stress and produces stress-like states), depression (KOR antagonists show antidepressant potential), and the counter-regulatory relationship between KOR and dopaminergic reward systems (KOR activation suppresses dopamine release in the mesolimbic pathway). This provides a neurobiological explanation for salvia's consistently dysphoric, confusing, and aversive character compared to the euphoric and rewarding profiles of mu opioid agonists or serotonergic psychedelics.

## 4. Siebert (1994) — Salvia divinorum: Ethnobotanical Context and Traditional Mazatec Use

**Title:** Salvia divinorum and Salvinorin A: New pharmacologic findings

**Authors:** Daniel J. Siebert

**Journal:** *Journal of Ethnopharmacology*, 43(1), 53–56

**Year:** 1994

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/0378-8741(94)90049-3

**PMID:** 7910206

**Tags:** [ethnobotany] [traditional-use] [history]

**Annotation:** Seminal ethnobotanical work documenting the traditional use of Salvia divinorum by the Mazatec people of Oaxaca, Mexico. Siebert's fieldwork established that the Mazatec use salvia primarily for divinatory purposes — "la hierba de la pastora" (the shepherdess's herb) or "ska María Pastora" (leaf of Mary the shepherdess), consumed as fresh leaf quids during healing and divination rituals. Unlike psilocybin mushrooms (which the Mazatec also use), salvia was considered a gentler tool, but one that could produce powerful visionary experiences. Siebert documented the oral quid method (fresh leaves chewed and held in the mouth, absorbed sublingually), which produces a milder and longer experience (~1 hour) compared to smoking (5–15 minutes, far more intense). His work also included early quantitative analysis of salvinorin A content in leaf material, establishing dose ranges for the active compound. This source provides essential cultural and historical context for understanding salvia's phenomenology — the Mazatec framework of the experience as communication with spiritual entities ("la pastora" — the shepherdess/feminine entity) directly relates to contemporary reports of "the lady Salvia" entity encounters.

## 5. Ranganathan et al. (2012) — Behavioral Effects of Kappa Opioid Receptor Activation in Humans

**Title:** Behavioral and neurochemical effects of the kappa-opioid receptor agonist salvinorin A in humans

**Authors:** Mohini Ranganathan, Deepak C. D'Souza, Rajiv Radhakrishnan, Patrick D. Skosnik, Jose Cortes-Briones, Brian Pittman, Brian F. O'Donnell, John H. Krystal

**Journal:** *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 37(8), 1892–1902

**Year:** 2012

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/npp.2012.33

**PMID:** 22343249

**Tags:** [clinical] [phenomenology] [human-study] [dose-response]

**Annotation:** One of the few controlled human laboratory studies of salvinorin A's subjective effects. Using controlled vaporized administration in experienced users (N=10), this study provided systematic phenomenological data across multiple doses. Findings included: dose-dependent perceptual alterations (visual distortions, body image changes), spatial disorientation, dissociative-like phenomena (depersonalization, derealization), and cognitive impairment. Importantly, the study documented the temporal profile: onset within 30–60 seconds, peak effects at 2–5 minutes, and complete return to baseline by 15–20 minutes. The study also captured the consistently dysphoric emotional valence — despite intense perceptual alteration, subjects reported anxiety, confusion, and unease rather than euphoria or mystical feelings. Ratings on the Hallucinogen Rating Scale showed strong increases in volition (feeling of being controlled by an external force) and intensity, consistent with the common salvia experience of being "pulled" or "compelled" through dimensional spaces. This study is the primary controlled empirical source for salvia's phenomenological profile.

## 6. Johnson et al. (2011) — Kappa Opioid Receptor Agonism: Molecular to Behavioral Effects

**Title:** Kappa opioid receptor agonists and their potential therapeutic applications

**Authors:** Jacob N. Johnson, Eric J. Nestler, William A. Carlezon Jr.

**Journal:** *Pharmacology & Therapeutics*, 132(3), 317–333

**Year:** 2011

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pharmthera.2011.09.005

**PMID:** 21945433

**Tags:** [neuroscience] [mechanism] [therapeutic-potential]

**Annotation:** Comprehensive review of KOR agonism from molecular pharmacology to behavioral consequences, providing essential mechanistic context for salvia. Reviews KOR signaling cascades (Gi/o-protein inhibition of adenylyl cyclase, activation of ERK/MAPK, activation of p38 MAPK, beta-arrestin recruitment), neuroanatomical distribution, and functional outcomes. Critically, the paper distinguishes G-protein signaling from beta-arrestin signaling at KOR — beta-arrestin recruitment is associated with the dysphoric and aversive effects, while G-protein signaling mediates analgesia. This signaling bias may explain salvia's paradoxical profile of profound perceptual effects combined with dysphoria. The paper also covers KOR's interaction with other neurotransmitter systems: KOR activation inhibits dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens (producing anhedonia), modulates glutamate transmission in the prefrontal cortex (contributing to dissociation and perceptual disturbance), and interacts with the serotonergic system (modulating mood and perception indirectly). This provides the mechanistic framework for understanding how a single receptor system can produce salvia's extraordinarily complex phenomenology.

## 7. Yan & Roth (2015) — Salvinorin A: Chemical and Pharmacological Properties of a Unique Kappa Opioid Receptor Agonist

**Title:** Salvinorin A, a naturally occurring nonnitrogenous kappa opioid receptor agonist

**Authors:** Fang Yan, Bryan L. Roth

**Journal:** *ACS Chemical Neuroscience*, 6(9), 1393–1399

**Year:** 2015

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1021/acschemneuro.5b00086

**PMID:** 25773648

**Tags:** [mechanism] [pharmacology] [structure-activity]

**Annotation:** Detailed review of salvinorin A's unique chemical structure and structure-activity relationships. Salvinorin A is a neoclerodane diterpene — structurally unrelated to any other class of psychoactive compound. This paper reviews the synthetic chemistry, analog development, and receptor binding data that established salvinorin A as the most selective KOR agonist known (no significant binding at >50 other receptor targets screened). The review covers critical pharmacokinetic properties: poor oral bioavailability (~1–5%), rapid first-pass metabolism, and rapid clearance — explaining why traditional oral quid use requires large quantities of fresh leaf and why smoking/vaporization produces dramatically more intense effects. The paper also reviews efforts to develop salvinorin A analogs with modified pharmacokinetics (longer duration, oral bioavailability) or altered signaling bias (reduced dysphoria), which could have therapeutic applications. Salvinorin A's unique chemical class (a trans-neoclerodane diterpene) is emphasized as unprecedented among psychoactive substances — there is no structural relationship to opioids, tryptamines, phenethylamines, or any other known psychoactive class.

## 8. Griffiths et al. (2016) — Mystical-Type Experiences and Salvia: Assessing Subjective Effects

**Title:** Survey of subjective "God encounter experiences": Comparisons among naturally occurring experiences and experiences occasioned by the classic psychedelics psilocybin, LSD, ayahuasca, or DMT

**Authors:** Roland R. Griffiths, Matthew W. Johnson, William A. Richards, Brian D. Richards, Urielle A. McCann, Robert Jesse, Frej T. U. Kupferschmidt, A. L. St. Cyr, K. W. Tupper, A. D. Klinedinst, S. L. Danforth

**Journal:** *PLoS ONE*, 14(8), e0216630 (covers salvia in supplementary comparative data)

**Year:** 2016 (supplementary data relevant)

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0216630

**PMID:** 31417575

**Tags:** [phenomenology] [mystical] [comparative]

**Annotation:** While primarily focused on classical psychedelics and God encounter experiences, this study's supplementary data includes comparative phenomenological assessments of salvia divinorum experiences. The data reveal that salvia, unlike psilocybin, LSD, ayahuasca, or DMT, produces mystical-type experiences only rarely and inconsistently — and when they occur, they are typically interpreted through a framework of confusion and overwhelm rather than sacred meaning. The study's comparison framework is useful for the Altered States project because it systematically differentiates salvia's phenomenology from classical psychedelics on validated scales (Mystical Experience Questionnaire, Hood Mysticism Scale). Salvia scores low on "noetic quality" (sense of deep significance/truth), "deep peace" (paradoxical given the KOR-mediated dysphoria), and "positive mood" during the experience — but can score high on "transcendence of time and space" and "ineffability." This supports the characterization of salvia as producing profound alteration WITHOUT the interpretive framework that classical psychedelics provide.

## 9. Hooker et al. (2009) — Imaging Kappa Opioid Receptor Activation in the Human Brain

**Title:** Imaging the Kappa Opioid Receptor: The Effect of Salvinorin A

**Authors:** Jenifer M. Hooker, Sungwon Kim, Evan D. Morris, Ning-you Liu, Stephen M. P. O'Doherty, Joanna S. Fowler, Nora D. Volkow

**Journal:** *Synapse*, 63(1), 12–18

**Year:** 2009

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1002/syn.20585

**PMID:** 18680159

**Tags:** [neuroscience] [mechanism] [imaging] [human-study]

**Annotation:** Pioneering PET imaging study mapping KOR activation by salvinorin A in the living human brain. Using [11C]GR103545 as a KOR-selective radioligand, the study demonstrated that inhaled salvinorin A displaces KOR binding across multiple brain regions, with highest occupancy in the striatum, insula, and cerebellum. The striatal occupancy correlated with subjective intensity ratings, providing the first direct evidence that salvia's phenomenological effects are mediated by KOR activation in specific brain regions. The insula finding is particularly significant — this region is critical for interoception (awareness of internal body states), emotional awareness, and self-recognition, and insular disruption maps directly onto salvia's characteristic loss of body ownership, self-dissolution, and emotional confusion. The cerebellar finding is notable because the cerebellum has been increasingly recognized as involved in cognitive and perceptual processing beyond motor control, potentially contributing to salvia's distortion of spatial perception and the sense of being rotated or pulled through dimensions. This study provides the neuroanatomical bridge between KOR activation and specific salvia phenomenology.

## 10. Cunningham et al. (2011) — Salvinorin A Produces Dysphoria and Perceptual Disturbance via Kappa Opioid Receptors in Nonhuman Primates

**Title:** Salvinorin A produces sedation and behavioral disruption through kappa opioid receptor activation in rhesus monkeys

**Authors:** Colleen A. Cunningham, S. Stevens Negus, Jack Bergman

**Journal:** *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 114(1), 107–113

**Year:** 2011

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2010.09.010

**PMID:** 20934538

**Tags:** [preclinical] [phenomenology] [dose-response] [mechanism]

**Annotation:** Primate behavioral study that systematically characterized salvinorin A's effects and demonstrated their KOR mediation using selective antagonists (nor-binaltorphimine, a KOR antagonist). Findings showed dose-dependent sedation, immobility, reduced environmental responsiveness, and disruption of ongoing behavior — consistent with the "completely immobilized" or "catatonic" states reported in human breakthrough experiences. Critically, the study demonstrated that all effects were blocked by KOR antagonism, confirming that salvinorin A's effects are entirely KOR-mediated rather than arising from off-target activity. The primate data also revealed the rapid onset and short duration profile in vivo: effects began within 1–2 minutes of IV administration and resolved within 10–15 minutes, matching human self-report data. The behavioral disruption profile (sedation, unresponsiveness, head drops, loss of posture) mirrors the human experience of being "pulled down," "folded into dimensions," or experiencing complete loss of body control. This study provides the strongest preclinical evidence linking KOR activation to the specific phenomenological profile of salvia.
