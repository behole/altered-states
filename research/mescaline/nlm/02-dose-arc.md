The oral mescaline experience follows a prolonged timeline that typically lasts between **8 to 14 hours**, making it the longest-acting of the classic psychedelics [1-3]. This extended duration fundamentally defines the structure of the journey. 

**The Dose Arc and Timeline**
*   **Onset (0 to 1–2 hours):** Mescaline has a notoriously slow onset. It can take 45 to 90 minutes—and often up to 2 hours—for the drug to absorb and take effect, a delay tied to its relatively weak potency at 5-HT2A receptors [2, 4, 5]. 
*   **Ascent (2 to 3 hours):** Following the initial onset latency, the effects steadily build as the user transitions out of the somatic discomfort [3]. 
*   **Peak (2 to 5 hours):** The most intense subjective and visual effects manifest in this window [1, 2]. 
*   **Plateau (3 to 8 hours):** Mescaline creates a massive, sustained plateau rather than a sharp peak and quick drop-off [3]. Chemist Alexander Shulgin famously described this extended period as granting **"hours of sustained wonder, like standing in a cathedral made of light"** [6].
*   **Descent (8 to 12+ hours):** The experience slowly fades over several hours, with standard doses taking 10 to 12 hours to conclude, and some ceremonial or larger doses extending up to 14 hours [2, 3, 7].

**How the Duration Shapes the Arc**
Because it is the longest-acting substance of its class, the 10 to 14-hour duration makes **arc management critical** [1, 3]. Unlike psilocybin’s shorter 6-hour arc, mescaline produces a sustained, all-night or all-day state [8]. This requires significant physical and mental endurance; participants must learn to navigate, surrender, and sustain their focus over many hours [8]. The extended timeline means the experience is less about quick, transient shifts and more about settling into a prolonged, stable, and deeply immersive journey.

**The Role of the Nausea Phase**
The ascent of mescaline is dominated by an intense, nearly universal phase of nausea during the first 1 to 2 hours [1, 3]. 
*   **Pharmacological Reality:** This nausea is a direct pharmacological effect of the substance, not merely a psychological reaction [2]. In surveys, roughly 68% to 80% of users report experiencing it, and 35% experience actual vomiting [1, 7].
*   **Thematic Function:** This somatic hurdle plays a major role in shaping the psychological arc of the experience. Culturally, particularly in ceremonial peyote use, the nausea is often embraced as a necessary physical and spiritual "cleansing" [8]. It acts as a gatekeeper phase, sometimes framed colloquially as **"the cactus testing you"** before allowing the user to access the profound, visually rich plateau that follows [3].
