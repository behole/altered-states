The visual character of mescaline is distinctively bright, structural, and deeply connected to natural forms. Users consistently describe the imagery as featuring **"colors of extraordinary brilliance"** and complex patterns that are frequently characterized as **"kaleidoscopic," "tribal," "Aztec," or "Mayan"** in motif [1-3]. The aesthetic is highly **ornamental** and defined by an **"organic geometry"** where nature imagery is highly prominent [1, 4, 5].

When compared to the classic tryptamine psychedelics, mescaline occupies a unique visual middle ground:
*   **Versus LSD:** Mescaline's patterns are **more earthy, organic, and "alive" compared to the highly precise, electric, and geometric fractals** characteristic of LSD [3, 6, 7]. 
*   **Versus Psilocybin:** While psilocybin is known for flowing, morphing visual effects, mescaline's imagery is **more highly structured, vivid, and ornamental** [3, 6, 7]. 

Because of this unique blend of organic earthiness and complex, colorful structure, the most appropriate metaphor domains for capturing mescaline visuals include **mandalas, intricate tilework, weaving, kaleidoscopes, or a "painted desert"** [3].
