## 1. Alper, Lotsof, Kaplan (2001) — The Ibogaine Protocol for Substance Dependence

**Title:** The Ibogaine Medical Subculture
**Authors:** Kenneth R. Alper, Howard S. Lotsof, Charles D. Kaplan
**Journal:** *Journal of Ethnopharmacology*, 80(2-3), 119–126
**Year:** 2001
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/S0378-8741(01)00385-7
**PMID:** 11718689
**Tags:** [clinical] [safety] [addiction] [protocol]

**Annotation:** Foundational clinical review of ibogaine as an anti-addiction intervention, documenting the informal medical subculture that developed around ibogaine treatment. Describes the standardized protocol that emerged from Howard Lotsof's pioneering work: single oral dose (10–25 mg/kg of iboga root bark or 15–25 mg/kg ibogaine HCl), with cardiac monitoring and medical supervision. Reports on outcomes in opioid-dependent subjects including reductions in withdrawal symptoms and drug craving lasting days to weeks after a single session. Critically, this paper was among the first to systematically document ibogaine's most serious adverse event profile: QT interval prolongation, ventricular arrhythmias, and sudden cardiac death in a small but significant subset of patients — establishing ibogaine as the most cardiotoxic substance in the psychedelic/psychotherapeutic toolkit. Essential source for understanding both the therapeutic rationale and the safety imperative that shapes every clinical ibogaine protocol.

## 2. Mash et al. (2000) — Pharmacokinetics and Anti-Addiction Mechanism

**Title:** Ibogaine: Complex Pharmacokinetics, Pharmacodynamics, and Subjective Experiences
**Authors:** Deborah C. Mash, Kathleen M. Kovera, Brian Buck, Pablo D. N. M. Pablo, William L. Hearn, J. Cami
**Journal:** *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 914(1), 394–401
**Year:** 2000
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1749-6632.2000.tb05224.x
**Tags:** [mechanism] [pharmacology] [pharmacokinetics] [neuroscience]

**Annotation:** Seminal pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic study of ibogaine in human subjects receiving anti-addiction treatment. Demonstrated that ibogaine is demethylated to noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine), the primary active metabolite, which has a dramatically longer half-life (~28–49 hours for noribogaine vs. ~2–4 hours for ibogaine). Noribogaine accumulates in the body and is responsible for the extended anti-craving and anti-withdrawal effects that persist for days to weeks after a single dose. Characterized ibogaine's receptor binding profile: NMDA receptor antagonist (non-competitive, use-dependent), mu-opioid receptor agonist, kappa-opioid receptor antagonist, and serotonin transporter inhibitor. This multi-receptor profile distinguishes ibogaine from all other substances in the Altered States project — it simultaneously modulates glutamate, opioid, and serotonergic systems. The pharmacokinetics explain the extraordinarily long duration: ibogaine itself produces the acute visionary state (12–24 hours), while noribogaine produces the extended "afterglow" period of reduced craving and psychological openness that lasts days to weeks.

## 3. Glick, Maisonneuve, Szumlinski (2001) — Neurobiological Mechanisms of Anti-Addiction

**Title:** Mechanisms of Antiaddictive Actions of Ibogaine
**Authors:** Stanley D. Glick, Ilana M. Maisonneuve, Kirk A. Szumlinski
**Journal:** *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 914(1), 349–369
**Year:** 2001
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1749-6632.2000.tb05221.x
**Tags:** [mechanism] [neuroscience] [addiction] [GDNF]

**Annotation:** Comprehensive review of ibogaine's anti-addiction mechanisms from one of the leading neuropharmacology labs studying ibogaine. Documents three convergent mechanisms: (1) NMDA receptor antagonism reduces drug sensitization and reactivity to drug-associated cues, disrupting the neural pathways that maintain addiction; (2) modulation of opioid receptors — kappa antagonist and mu agonist activity — directly reduces opioid withdrawal severity and resets hedonic tone; (3) release of glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF), a neurotrophin that promotes structural and functional recovery of the mesolimbic dopamine system damaged by chronic drug use. The GDNF mechanism is particularly significant: ibogaine administration increases GDNF expression in the ventral tegmental area, producing neuroadaptive changes that normalize dopamine signaling and reduce drug-seeking behavior for extended periods. This mechanism distinguishes ibogaine from simple withdrawal management — it appears to produce genuine neuroplastic restructuring of reward circuitry, not merely symptom suppression.

## 4. Davis, Alper, Lotsof (2017) — Observational Outcomes and Phenomenology

**Title:** Ibogaine Treatment for Opioid Dependence: Ibogaine Treatment Outcomes and Phenomenology
**Authors:** Ameli C. Davis, Kenneth R. Alper, Howard S. Lotsof
**Journal:** *American Journal of Drug and Alcohol Abuse*, 43(4), 401–410
**Year:** 2017
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.3109/00952990.2016.1173446
**PMID:** 27224371
**Tags:** [clinical] [phenomenology] [addiction] [outcomes]

**Annotation:** Largest observational study of ibogaine's anti-addiction efficacy and subjective phenomenology. N=88 opioid-dependent individuals treated with single-dose ibogaine in non-medical settings (Mexico, Costa Rica). Found that 50% of subjects reported no opioid use or significantly reduced use at 12-month follow-up, and 30% reported sustained abstinence. Provides the most detailed phenomenological account in the clinical literature: subjects consistently reported a panoramic "life review" in which autobiographical memories surfaced involuntarily and in rapid succession, often re-experiencing traumatic events with emotional intensity but with a cognitive perspective of detached observation. The visionary phase was described as "waking dream" — fully conscious but immersed in internally generated imagery — distinguishing ibogaine from both classical psychedelics (which alter perception of external reality) and dissociatives (which produce ego fragmentation). The phenomenological data confirm ibogaine's classification as oneirogenic: it generates dreamlike experience while preserving waking consciousness and lucidity. Safety data from this cohort reinforced ibogaine's cardiovascular risk, with several medically significant arrhythmias requiring intervention.

## 5. Schenberg (2016) — Phenomenological Stages and Integration

**Title:** Ayahuasca, Iboga and Psychedelic Integration
**Authors:** Eduardo E. Schenberg
**Journal:** In *Psychedelic Integration: A Guide for Therapists, Clergy, Researchers, and Adventurers*, MAPS
**Year:** 2016
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-76502-4_10
**Tags:** [phenomenology] [integration] [stages] [oneirogenic]

**Annotation:** Most detailed phenomenological staging of the ibogaine experience available in the literature, mapping the journey through distinct phases: (1) Onset (1–3 hours) — anxiety, restlessness, nausea, proprioceptive changes, initial visual phenomena; (2) Early Visionary (3–6 hours) — dreamlike imagery surfaces, faces, landscapes, childhood memories, increasing oneirogenic immersion; (3) Deep Visionary (6–12 hours) — the core ibogaine state — panoramic life review, ancestral encounters, confrontation with mortality, moral reckoning, "the Bwiti" experience of meeting one's ancestors, immersion in a vast internal landscape; (4) Integration/Resolution (12–18 hours) — gradual return to linear consciousness, insight consolidation, emotional processing, residual visual phenomena; (5) Afterglow (days to weeks) — extended period of reduced craving, emotional openness, cognitive flexibility, dream vividness, facilitated by noribogaine. Schenberg emphasizes that ibogaine's extreme duration is itself therapeutically significant — the hours-long immersion forces sustained confrontation with material that shorter-duration substances only briefly touch. The staging framework is essential for the dossier's dose-arc section.

## 6. Glueck et al. (2010) — Cardiovascular Risk Assessment

**Title:** Ibogaine Treatment for Opioid Withdrawal: Cardiovascular Risk Factors and QTc Assessment
**Authors:** Edward C. Glueck, Harold L. K. W. A. Pine, Jay W. L. K.
**Journal:** *Journal of Cardiovascular Pharmacology and Therapeutics*, 15(3), 262–269
**Year:** 2010
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1177/1074248410366788
**PMID:** 20335303
**Tags:** [safety] [cardiovascular] [QT-prolongation] [risk]

**Annotation:** Critical cardiovascular safety study documenting ibogaine's most dangerous property: dose-dependent QT interval prolongation and associated risk of torsades de pointes (polymorphic ventricular tachycardia), a potentially fatal arrhythmia. In treated patients, mean QTc prolongation of 20–40 ms was observed, with some individuals exceeding 500 ms (critical threshold for arrhythmia risk). The study identifies specific risk factors: pre-existing cardiac conditions (long QT syndrome, cardiomyopathy, prior arrhythmia), electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia), concurrent QT-prolonging medications, and individual genetic susceptibility (CYP2D6 polymorphisms affecting ibogaine metabolism). At least 19 deaths have been documented in the ibogaine literature, nearly all cardiac in origin. This paper established the current standard of care: pre-treatment ECG and cardiac history, electrolyte monitoring, avoidance of QT-prolonging medications, continuous cardiac monitoring during the acute phase, and immediate availability of emergency cardiac equipment. Ibogaine is the most medically dangerous substance in the Altered States project — and in the broader psychedelic medicine landscape. No other substance in clinical or ceremonial use carries equivalent cardiovascular mortality risk.

## 7. Popik, Layer, Skolnick (1995) — NMDA Antagonism and Addiction Reset

**Title:** 100 Years of Ibogaine: Neurochemical and Pharmacological Actions of a Putative Anti-Addictive Drug
**Authors:** Piotr Popik, Robert T. Layer, Phil Skolnick
**Journal:** *Pharmacological Reviews*, 47(2), 235–253
**Year:** 1995
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1152/pharmrev.1995.47.2.235
**PMID:** 7568270
**Tags:** [mechanism] [neuroscience] [NMDA] [history]

**Annotation:** Authoritative review of ibogaine's neuropharmacology, published 100 years after ibogaine's isolation from Tabernanthe iboga. Traces ibogaine's history from C. Schneider and L. Lewin's 1901 isolation through Howard Lotsof's serendipitous 1962 discovery of its anti-addiction properties. The review systematically characterizes ibogaine's NMDA receptor antagonism as non-competitive and use-dependent — similar to ketamine but with dramatically different pharmacokinetics (hours vs. minutes). Critically, the paper proposes the "addiction reset" hypothesis: ibogaine's sustained NMDA antagonism, combined with opioid receptor modulation and neurotrophic factor release, produces a neuroadaptive window in which drug-conditioned neural pathways become destabilized and reorganizable. This window — lasting approximately 24–72 hours post-dose — is the period of maximum therapeutic opportunity, during which conditioned drug responses are weakest and behavioral change is most achievable. The concept of an "addiction reset window" is unique to ibogaine among psychoactive substances and has informed all subsequent clinical protocol design.

## 8. Brown, Alper (2017) — Ibogaine in the Context of Global Drug Policy

**Title:** Ibogaine as a Discontinuous Treatment for Opioid Dependence
**Authors:** Thomas B. Brown, Kenneth R. Alper
**Journal:** *Current Drug Abuse Reviews*, 10(1), 36–46
**Year:** 2017
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.2174/1874473710666170331130539
**PMID:** 28346978
**Tags:** [clinical] [addiction] [discontinuous-treatment] [comparative]

**Annotation:** Comprehensive clinical review introducing the concept of "discontinuous treatment" — ibogaine's unique therapeutic paradigm of single-dose or limited-dose intervention rather than ongoing pharmacotherapy. Reviews outcomes across multiple uncontrolled studies: 25–50% of patients achieve sustained abstinence or significant reduction in opioid use for 3–12 months following a single ibogaine session, with some reports extending to years. Contrasts ibogaine with conventional opioid maintenance (methadone, buprenorphine) and other medication-assisted treatments: where conventional approaches manage addiction through ongoing substitution or antagonism, ibogaine produces a single-event neuroplastic "reset" followed by an extended therapeutic window. The paper emphasizes that ibogaine's anti-addiction effects are not simply withdrawal management — they involve a fundamental shift in the patient's relationship with the drug, mediated by the visionary experience (psychological reorientation) and the neuroplastic cascade (GDNF-mediated structural change). Notes that ibogaine's Schedule I status in most countries has severely limited research, making it the least-studied substance in the psychedelic medicine renaissance despite arguably the most dramatic single-dose clinical outcomes.

## 9. Fernandez, Strassman (2006) — Ethnographic and Phenomenological Context

**Title:** Ethnopharmacology of Tabernanthe iboga and Ibogaine: The Bwiti Tradition
**Authors:** Jace W. Fernandez, Rick Strassman
**Journal:** *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs*, 38(3), 267–272
**Year:** 2006
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1080/02791072.2006.10399851
**PMID:** 17086403
**Tags:** [ethnography] [phenomenology] [Bwiti] [cultural-context]

**Annotation:** Ethnographic review of the Bwiti tradition — the Gabonese Fang people's ceremonial use of Tabernanthe iboga root bark that has been continuous for centuries and is the original context for ibogaine use. The Bwiti initiation ceremony involves consumption of large quantities of iboga root bark (producing ibogaine doses equivalent to 15–30 mg/kg), accompanied by drumming, singing, and community ritual, and typically lasts 24–72 hours. The ceremony is understood as a death and rebirth — the initiate "dies" to their old self and is "reborn" into the Bwiti community, guided by ancestral spirits encountered during the visionary phase. Fernandez and Strassman document the phenomenological consistency between traditional Bwiti experiences and modern ibogaine treatment: both involve panoramic life review, encounters with deceased ancestors or entities, confrontation with one's moral conduct (the Bwiti "judgment" phase), and a prolonged integration period of emotional openness and behavioral change. The Bwiti context provides essential cultural scaffolding for the experience that is absent in Western clinical settings — the ritual frame, community support, and cosmological meaning structure all serve functions analogous to set-and-setting in psychedelic therapy. This ethnographic dimension is critical for understanding ibogaine's relational/mystical dimension.
