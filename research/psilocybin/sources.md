# Psilocybin — Annotated Source List

> **Project:** Altered States Skill Project
> **Last updated:** 2026-04-04
> **Sources:** 9 (6 peer-reviewed, 2 review/theoretical, 1 first-person account)

---

## 1. Griffiths et al. (2016) — Cancer Patients RCT

**Title:** Psilocybin produces substantial and sustained decreases in depression and anxiety in patients with life-threatening cancer: A randomized double-blind trial
**Authors:** Roland R. Griffiths, Matthew W. Johnson, Michael A. Carducci, Annie Umbricht, William A. Richards, Brian D. Richards, Mary P. Cosimano, Margaret A. Klinedinst
**Journal:** *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 30(12), 1181–1197
**Year:** 2016
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5367557/
**Tags:** [clinical] [phenomenology] [dose-response]

**Annotation:** Landmark RCT (N=51) demonstrating that a single high-dose psilocybin session (22 mg/70 kg) vs. active placebo (1 mg/70 kg) produces mystical experiences in ~76% of cancer patients, with rapid and sustained antidepressant/anxiolytic effects at 6-month follow-up. Provides critical dose-response data and establishes that mystical experience intensity mediates therapeutic outcome. Used 5D-ASC, MEQ, HRS instruments.

## 2. Griffiths et al. (2006) — Original Mystical Experience Study

**Title:** Psilocybin can occasion mystical-type experiences having substantial and sustained personal meaning and spiritual significance
**Authors:** Roland R. Griffiths, William A. Richards, Una McCann, Robert Jesse
**Journal:** *Psychopharmacology*, 187(3), 268–283
**Year:** 2006
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16826400/
**Tags:** [clinical] [phenomenology] [dose-response]

**Annotation:** The first rigorously controlled modern study showing psilocybin (30 mg/70 kg vs. methylphenidate control) reliably produces mystical-type experiences in a laboratory setting (N=36, hallucinogen-naïve). 61% met criteria for "complete mystical experience" on the Hood Mysticism Scale. 67% rated it among the top 5 most meaningful experiences of their lives. Established the foundational phenomenological taxonomy: oceanic boundlessness, ego dissolution, visionary restructuralization.

## 3. Griffiths et al. (2008) — 14-Month Follow-Up

**Title:** Mystical-type experiences occasioned by psilocybin mediate the attribution of personal meaning and spiritual significance 14 months later
**Authors:** Roland R. Griffiths, William A. Richards, Matthew W. Johnson, Una D. McCann, Robert Jesse
**Journal:** *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 22(6), 621–632
**Year:** 2008
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3050654/
**Tags:** [clinical] [phenomenology]

**Annotation:** 14-month follow-up of the 2006 cohort demonstrating that the subjective significance of psilocybin-induced mystical experiences does not diminish over time — 67% still rated it a top-5 life experience. Community observers independently confirmed positive behavioral changes. The mediation finding (acute mystical experience intensity predicts long-term positive change) is foundational for understanding why phenomenological quality matters therapeutically.

## 4. Barrett et al. (2022) — Mystical Experiences Quant/Qual Report

**Title:** Psilocybin-Induced Mystical-Type Experiences are Related to Persisting Positive Effects: A Quantitative and Qualitative Report
**Authors:** Frederick S. Barrett, Theresa M. Carbonaro, Ethan Hurwitz, Matthew W. Johnson, Roland R. Griffiths
**Journal:** *Frontiers in Pharmacology*, 13, 841648
**Year:** 2022
**URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pharmacology/articles/10.3389/fphar.2022.841648/full
**Tags:** [phenomenology] [clinical] [first-person]

**Annotation:** The richest phenomenological data in this set. Mixed-methods study (N=75, pooled JHU studies) combining MEQ30 quantitative scores with open-ended qualitative thematic analysis. Identifies recurring motifs: unity, love, visual complexity, death/rebirth, ineffability, temporal transcendence. Demonstrates that specifically the *mystical quality* — not mere perceptual intensity — predicts lasting positive transformation.

## 5. Metastasio & Prevete (2025) — Phenomenology Review

**Title:** The phenomenology of psilocybin: transformative insights for research and clinical practice
**Authors:** Antonio Metastasio, Elisabeth Prevete
**Journal:** *Frontiers in Psychology*, 16, 1455902
**Year:** 2025
**URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1455902/full
**Tags:** [phenomenology] [dose-response]

**Annotation:** The most directly relevant paper for the project — a comprehensive cross-dimensional review synthesizing perceptual, temporal, cognitive, emotional, ego, mystical, and somatic effects into a single organized framework. Crucially provides dose-dependent phenomenological progression: low doses produce mild perceptual enhancement and color intensification; high doses produce complex visionary experiences, complete ego dissolution, and temporal transcendence. The ideal reference architecture for mapping the 7 phenomenological dimensions.

## 6. Stoliker, Egan & Razi (2022) — REBUS/Predictive Coding Framework

**Title:** Reduced Precision Underwrites Ego Dissolution and Therapeutic Outcomes Under Psychedelics
**Authors:** Devon Stoliker, Gary F. Egan, Adeel Razi
**Journal:** *Frontiers in Neuroscience*, 16, 827400
**Year:** 2022
**URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnins.2022.827400/full
**Tags:** [neuroscience] [phenomenology]

**Annotation:** Theoretical review providing the mechanistic framework (REBUS/free energy principle) for psilocybin phenomenology. Argues that 5-HT2A agonism reduces the precision of high-level Bayesian beliefs, which directly explains ego dissolution, altered perception, and therapeutic efficacy through a single computational mechanism. Essential for grounding the "why" behind the phenomenological shifts — the loosening of rigidly held self-models enables access to alternative ways of making sense of the world.

## 7. Lebedev et al. (2015) — Neural Correlates of Ego Dissolution

**Title:** Finding the Self by Losing the Self: Neural Correlates of Ego-Dissolution Under Psilocybin
**Authors:** Alexander V. Lebedev, Martin Lovden, Gidon Rosenthal, Amanda Feilding, David J. Nutt, Robin L. Carhart-Harris
**Journal:** *Human Brain Mapping*, 36(8), 3137–3153
**Year:** 2015
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26010878/
**Tags:** [neuroscience] [phenomenology]

**Annotation:** Landmark fMRI study (N=15, 2 mg IV psilocybin, placebo-controlled) providing first direct neural correlates of ego dissolution: MTL-cortical decoupling, salience network disintegration, and reduced interhemispheric communication. Demonstrates that the sense of self depends on specific network coupling patterns, and psilocybin systematically disrupts these. Individual susceptibility varies based on baseline executive network diversity.

## 8. Stoliker, Preller, Novelli et al. (2024) — Neural Mechanisms of Visual Imagery

**Title:** Neural Mechanisms of Psychedelic Visual Imagery
**Authors:** Devon Stoliker, Katrin H. Preller, Leonardo Novelli, Alan Anticevic, Gary F. Egan, Franz X. Vollenweider, Adeel Razi
**Journal:** *Molecular Psychiatry*, 30(4), 1259–1266
**Year:** 2024
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-024-02632-3
**Tags:** [neuroscience] [phenomenology]

**Annotation:** Empirical fMRI/DCM study investigating the neural basis of psilocybin's most prominent perceptual feature: eyes-closed visual imagery. Demonstrates that psilocybin shifts effective connectivity in visual pathways toward top-down feedback dominance — the brain "sees" its own internal imagery as if it were external visual input. Explains why users experience vivid geometric patterns, landscapes, and entity encounters with eyes closed. Maps the visual hierarchy: V1 -> fusiform gyrus -> intraparietal sulcus -> inferior frontal gyrus.

## 9. Cosimano (2014) — First-Person Guide Account

**Title:** My Experience as a Guide in the Johns Hopkins Psilocybin Research Project (subtitle: "Love: The Nature of Our True Self")
**Author:** Mary Cosimano, M.S.W.
**Source:** *MAPS Bulletin*, Vol. 24, No. 3 (Winter 2014), p. 39
**Year:** 2014
**URL:** https://maps.org/news/bulletin/my-experience-as-a-guide-in-the-johns-hopkins-psilocybin-research-project/
**Tags:** [first-person] [phenomenology]

**Annotation:** Unique qualitative account from the primary session guide across all Johns Hopkins psilocybin studies (~350+ sessions). Captures the experiential gestalt that quantitative instruments miss: love, interconnection, and authentic self-contact as the core emotional architecture of the psilocybin experience. Emphasizes the relational dimension — both internal (connecting with "true self" beneath ego) and interpersonal (the guide-participant therapeutic container). The consistent emergence of love as the dominant theme across hundreds of sessions is a powerful qualitative finding.
