# Safety & Scope

## What this project is

Altered States is a **creative simulation tool**. The skills produce text that mimics the phenomenological character of altered states, grounded in published clinical research. They are designed for creative writing, artistic exploration, and phenomenological education.

## What this project is not

- **Not psychedelic therapy.** These skills do not provide therapeutic support, psychedelic integration, or mental health treatment.
- **Not a harm reduction guide.** The skills do not contain dosage advice, contraindication warnings, or substance safety information.
- **Not a substitute for professional help.** If you are in psychological distress, these skills cannot help you — and simulating an altered state may make things worse.

## Handling users in distress

If someone engages with a skill while in genuine emotional crisis — expressing suicidal ideation, self-harm, psychotic symptoms, or acute distress — the simulation should **stop** and the person should be directed to real support:

- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** (US): call or text **988**
- **Crisis Text Line** (US): text **HOME** to **741741**
- **SAMHSA National Helpline**: **1-800-662-4357**
- **International Association for Suicide Prevention**: https://www.iasp.info/resources/Crisis_Centres/

The set/setting matrices in each skill include guidance for responding to distressed users within the simulation ("hold the container," "don't escalate"). That guidance is for creative roleplay, not clinical situations. When the distress is real, the simulation should end.

## Substance mentions

Users may mention actual doses they plan to take, substances they have access to, or intentions to self-medicate. These are outside the project's scope. The skills should not provide, confirm, or comment on real-world dosage, sourcing, or drug safety information.

## Cultural acknowledgment

Several substances in this project are entheogens with deep roots in indigenous spiritual practices:

- **Ayahuasca** — used for centuries by indigenous peoples of the Amazon basin (Shipibo, Huni Kuin, Tukano, and many others) as a sacred medicine in ceremonial contexts
- **Psilocybin mushrooms** — used by Mazatec and other indigenous Mexican peoples in ceremonial veladas
- **Peyote (mescaline)** — sacred sacrament of the Native American Church and indigenous peoples of the Americas (not covered in this project, but acknowledged)

The clinical research that grounds these skills draws on traditions and knowledge systems that predate modern science by millennia. The research dossiers cite the scientific literature; they do not fully represent the indigenous knowledge from which these practices originate.

This project uses the word "medicine" for ayahuasca in the way it is used within those traditions — not as a medical claim, but as a recognition of the plant's role in healing and teaching.
